The Evolution of Sports

This leads to how sports have evolved over time. Many sports in the ancient Greek world involved little to no accessories. For instance, there was no ball that the athletes would use. Many of the events forced the ancient athletes to use their bodies to their fullest potential. One event, however, used animals in order to compete. In the chariot racing event, athletes would ride a chariot with a set number of horses: “There were both 2-horse chariot and 4-horse chariot races, with separate races for chariots drawn by foals. Another race was between carts drawn by a team of 2 mules. The course was 12 laps around the stadium track (9 miles)” (Ancient Sports).

This is an example that shows that there was one event in the ancient Olympics where athletes used some sort of accessory in an event. In an event like this, an athlete demonstrates their skill more than their physical ability, which makes it similar to a modern event like basketball. Both chariot racing and basketball need some sort of physical ability, but both sports are more focused on the ability of the athlete to use the designated accessory.

Another distinction between modern and ancient Olympics is the rules of certain events. For instance, pankration would never be allowed in the modern Olympics because of how brutal and inhumane of a sport it was. In the ancient world, it was not seen as inhumane, which shows just how much the human race has evolved as a whole. People in the modern era are able to recognize when something is inhumane and stop it.
