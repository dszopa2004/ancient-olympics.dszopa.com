What Were the Ancient Olympics?

The ancient Olympics were a place where ancient Greek athletes could compete for glory and for fame. There are numerous examples to show how the ancient Greeks practically lived for glory. One of the most clear examples of this can be seen in Homer’s Odyssey. In the epic, Odysseus and his crewmates get stuck inside a cave with Polyphemus, a giant cyclops. Odysseus is able to outsmart by calling himself “Nobody” and blind Polyphemus and by getting him very drunk. As Odysseus and his crew are leaving the island, Odysseus yells for Polyphemus his name in order to make sure that Polyphemus knows who defeated him. This shows just how much the ancient Greeks cared about glory. Odysseus would have been able to get away with injuring the son of a god, but he had to ensure that Polyphemus knew who defeated him. The ancient Greeks were also able to bring fame to their city-states. According to Nigel B. Crowther, the athletes who were victorious were given prizes and prestige to their city: “Ancient cities rewarded well athletes who were victorious in the games and thereby brought prestige to the city…” (Crowther). This shows how ancient Greek athletes not only competed for themselves but also for their cities. This also shows how athletes were appreciated in the ancient world. If these athletes were not appreciated and welcomed, they would certainly not be rewarded for their victories. The ancient Olympics were hosted in many cities, and each city-state held the competitions in honor of certain gods. According to Sean and Colette Hemingway, the ancient Olympics were hosted in city-states such as “Delphi, Nemea, and Isthmia” (Hemingway). Each of these city-states honored different gods. For example, “the Pythian games at Delphi honored Apollo” (Hemingway), and “the games at Nemea honored Zeus” (Hemingway). This shows how the ancient Olympics may have varied from city to city.
